There's no shortage of AI tools out there. New ones pop up every week, and most of them are just thin wrappers around the same models with a fresh coat of paint.

But some of them are worth your time. I've tried a lot of these tools over the past year, and this is my honest take on the ones that actually made a difference in my workflow — not just sounded cool in a demo.

---

## AI Coding Assistants

### Claude Code (Anthropic)

**What it does:** AI-powered CLI coding assistant that lives in your terminal.

**What sets it apart:** Unlike browser-based AI, Claude Code works directly in your codebase. It reads your files, understands your project structure, runs commands, and writes code with full context. Think of it as pair programming with someone who already read your entire codebase.

**What I use it for:**
- Writing boilerplate code and tests
- Debugging complex issues (just paste the error)
- Refactoring across multiple files
- Writing commit messages and PR descriptions
- Understanding unfamiliar codebases quickly

**Pricing:** Based on API usage (Claude Pro subscription or API credits)

---

### GitHub Copilot

**What it does:** AI autocomplete directly in your IDE.

**Why it's still around:** Copilot has had competition, but its tight VS Code and JetBrains integration keeps it useful. The suggestions feel natural and the tab-to-accept workflow becomes muscle memory quickly.

**Good for:** Inline code completion, writing repetitive patterns, boilerplate generation.

**Pricing:** $10/month (Individual) | $19/month (Business)

---

### Cursor

**What it does:** AI-first code editor (VS Code fork) with deep AI integration.

**Why people like it:** The Cmd+K interface for AI-assisted editing is really well done. Select code, describe what you want to change in plain English, and it handles it. The "Composer" feature for multi-file edits is where it really shines.

**Good for:** Developers who want AI baked into their editor, not just autocomplete.

**Pricing:** Free tier available | $20/month (Pro)

---

## AI Writing and Content Tools

### Claude (Web/API)

**What it does:** Conversational AI for writing, research, analysis, and reasoning.

**Why I like it:** Claude is strong at long-form writing, nuanced analysis, and anything that requires careful thinking. It follows instructions well and the output reads naturally — less "AI-generated" than most alternatives.

**What I use it for:**
- Drafting blog posts and documentation
- Analyzing long documents and research papers
- Summarizing meeting notes
- Writing technical proposals
- Brainstorming and ideation

**Pricing:** Free tier | Claude Pro $20/month

---

### Notion AI

**What it does:** AI built directly into Notion for writing, summarizing, and organizing.

**Why it works:** The main advantage is context. Notion AI knows your workspace — your notes, docs, projects, and databases. When you ask it to summarize or draft something, it pulls from your actual data.

**What it's useful for:**
- Summarizing long meeting notes into action items
- Drafting project proposals from existing notes
- Translating content between languages
- Auto-filling database properties

**Pricing:** Included with Notion plans ($10/month+ per user)

---

## AI Design and Visual Tools

### Midjourney / DALL-E 3

**What they do:** Generate images from text descriptions.

**Practical uses for developers:**
- Creating blog post featured images (like this blog does)
- Generating placeholder assets for prototypes
- Creating social media graphics
- Designing logos and brand assets for side projects
- Generating diagrams and visual explanations

**When NOT to use:** Final production assets for clients. AI-generated images are great for personal projects and content, but clients often prefer custom design.

**Pricing:** Midjourney $10-60/month | DALL-E 3 via ChatGPT Plus $20/month

---

### Figma AI

**What it does:** AI-powered design features directly in Figma.

**Why developers should care:** Even if you're not a designer, Figma AI helps you put together decent mockups and prototypes. Auto-layout suggestions, component generation, and design-to-code features make it easier to go from idea to something visual.

**Good for:** Developer-designers, solo founders, rapid prototyping.

---

## AI Automation Tools

### Make (formerly Integromat)

**What it does:** Visual automation platform that connects apps and services.

**Why it's worth knowing about:** Make isn't just for non-technical users. Its visual workflow builder is faster than writing custom integration code for a lot of scenarios. Throw in the AI modules, and you can automate some pretty complex workflows without code.

**Automation examples:**
- New GitHub issue → AI summarizes → posts to Slack channel
- Customer email → AI categorizes → routes to correct team
- RSS feed → AI rewrites summary → posts to social media
- Form submission → AI scores lead → adds to CRM

**Pricing:** Free tier (1,000 ops) | $9/month (10,000 ops)

---

### n8n (Self-Hosted Alternative)

**What it does:** Open-source workflow automation (self-hosted Make alternative).

**Why developers pick it:** Full control, no vendor lock-in, and you can add custom code nodes. Self-hosting means unlimited operations for the cost of your server.

**Good for:** Developers who want automation without SaaS subscription costs.

**Pricing:** Free (self-hosted) | Cloud starts at $20/month

---

## AI Research and Knowledge Tools

### Perplexity AI

**What it does:** AI-powered search engine that provides sourced answers.

**Why it beats regular Google searches:** Instead of 10 blue links and SEO spam, Perplexity gives you a direct, sourced answer with citations. For technical research, it saves a lot of time.

**What I use it for:**
- Quick technical research ("What's the difference between gRPC and REST performance?")
- Checking if a library/tool is still maintained
- Finding best practices for specific technologies
- Fact-checking before writing articles

**Pricing:** Free | Pro $20/month (more follow-ups, better models)

---

### NotebookLM (Google)

**What it does:** AI research assistant that works with your documents.

**What makes it different:** Upload PDFs, docs, or paste URLs, and NotebookLM creates an AI assistant that ONLY uses your sources. No hallucination from general training data. It can even generate podcast-style audio summaries.

**Good for:** Research, studying documentation, understanding large codebases or RFCs.

**Pricing:** Free

## AI Productivity and Communication

### Granola

**What it does:** AI meeting notes that actually work.

**How it works:** Granola runs quietly during your meetings, captures the conversation, and produces structured notes with action items. Unlike tools that require a bot to join your call, Granola works locally.

**Good for:** Developers stuck in meetings who'd rather focus on the discussion than taking notes.

---

### Raycast AI (macOS)

**What it does:** AI assistant built into Raycast launcher for macOS.

**Why it's handy:** It's always one keystroke away. Ask questions, generate code, translate text, compose emails — all without leaving your current app. Because it's so quick to access, you end up using it way more than a browser-based tool.

**Pricing:** Included with Raycast Pro ($8/month)

---

## AI for DevOps and Infrastructure

### K8sGPT

**What it does:** AI-powered Kubernetes troubleshooting.

**Why it helps:** K8sGPT analyzes your cluster, identifies issues, and explains problems in plain English. Instead of parsing through kubectl output and event logs, you get clear explanations of what's wrong and how to fix it.

**Example:**
```
$ k8sgpt analyze

 Pod backend-api-7d8f9 in namespace production:
 ⚠️ CrashLoopBackOff - Container is repeatedly crashing.
 Root cause: Readiness probe failing on port 8080.
 The application takes ~45 seconds to start, but probe
 initialDelaySeconds is set to 10.
 Suggestion: Increase initialDelaySeconds to 60.
```

**Pricing:** Free (open source)

---

### Warp Terminal

**What it does:** Modern terminal with built-in AI command assistance.

**Why it's worth trying:** Stuck on a command? Describe what you want in English, and Warp suggests the right command. The AI also explains error messages and suggests fixes.

**Good for:** Developers who live in the terminal and want faster command discovery.

**Pricing:** Free (individual) | $15/month (team)

## How to Evaluate AI Tools (Without Getting Scammed)

The AI tool landscape is full of wrappers-on-wrappers and vaporware. Here's how to evaluate:

1. **Does it solve a problem you actually have?** Not a hypothetical one
2. **Can you measure the time saved?** If you can't, it's probably not saving time
3. **Is it faster than the manual alternative?** Some AI tools are slower than just doing it yourself
4. **What happens to your data?** Read the privacy policy, especially for code tools
5. **Is the free tier useful?** If they force you to pay before you can evaluate, be skeptical
6. **Is it a thin wrapper around ChatGPT?** If so, you can probably just use ChatGPT directly

## My Take

AI tools are useful in 2026 — no question. But you don't need 15 subscriptions. Most developers would do well with 3-4 tools that solve their biggest time drains.

The point isn't to automate everything. It's to **stop wasting time on tasks that don't need your brain**, so you can focus on the work that does.

If you haven't tried any of these, start with one. Use it for a week. If it saves you time, great — keep it. If not, drop it and try another one. No need to overcomplicate this.
